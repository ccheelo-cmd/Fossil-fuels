# Research Notes — The Case For Fossil Fuels (Steelman)

**Audience:** Same audience that saw my original "How Climate Change Affects the World We Live In" PBI deck.
**Position:** Not climate denial. Honest counter-argument grounded in energy poverty, justice, and engineering reality.
**Geographic anchor:** Zambia / sub-Saharan Africa.

---

## 1. The Scale of Energy Poverty — the human cost happening *right now*

### 1a. Electricity access
- **~600 million people in sub-Saharan Africa (47% of the population) had no electricity access in 2024.** Progress has stalled — fewer than 19M gained access in 2023 and 2024 vs 23M in 2019. Source: IEA, *Financing Electricity Access in Africa* (2025). https://www.iea.org/reports/financing-electricity-access-in-africa
- Globally, ~730 million people are without electricity access. Source: IEA commentary, https://www.iea.org/commentaries/access-to-electricity-stagnates-leaving-globally-730-million-in-the-dark
- ~220M people in Africa (40% of those without access) can't afford even the IEA "basic bundle" of 50–75 kWh/household/year — so affordability is as much a barrier as physical infrastructure.

### 1b. Clean cooking — the silent killer
- **~1 billion people in sub-Saharan Africa lack access to clean cooking** — roughly 4 in 5 households. Source: IEA, *Universal Access to Clean Cooking in Africa* (2024). https://www.iea.org/reports/universal-access-to-clean-cooking-in-africa
- This causes **~815,000 premature deaths per year in Africa alone** from household air pollution.
- Globally, ~2.1 billion people cook with biomass/kerosene/coal. WHO attributes **~3.2 million deaths per year** to household air pollution (most recent WHO estimate; the 2021 figure was 2.9M including 309,000 under-fives). Source: WHO Fact Sheet on Household Air Pollution. https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/household-air-pollution-and-health

> **Framing for the deck:** This is the number that flips the moral arithmetic. ~3.2 million deaths per year *today* from energy poverty is comparable in magnitude to the worst current annual estimates of deaths from climate change — and it falls on the same people climate change will hit hardest.

## 2. The Justice Gap — Africa's emissions vs the climate burden

- **Africa is responsible for <3% of cumulative global CO₂ emissions since 1750.** Sub-Saharan Africa specifically: **0.55%**. Source: Energy for Growth Hub, citing Our World in Data. https://energyforgrowth.org/article/sub-saharan-africa-emits-a-tiny-fraction-of-the-worlds-co2/
- Current annual emissions: Africa = **~3.9%** of global CO₂ from fossil fuels & industry (2021), despite hosting ~17% of the global population.
- **My original deck noted** that climate hazards fall hardest on developing nations and rural poor — those same nations *least responsible for the problem* are being told they can't use the cheapest historical path to industrialization.

> **Framing:** This is the "energy justice" or "development asymmetry" angle. Hits hardest because my original deck already showed the disparate impact slide — I'm extending the same logic.

## 3. The Zambia Story — the closest possible audience anchor

- **Only 54% of Zambians have access to the national grid** (80% urban, 34% rural). Only **~28% are actually connected**, and **just 4% report a reliable supply.** Source: Afrobarometer AD1178 (2025). https://www.afrobarometer.org/publication/ad1178-amid-electricity-crisis-zambians-favour-ending-state-monopoly-investing-in-solar-and-wind-power/
- **85% of Zambia's ~3,985 MW installed capacity is hydropower.** Source: Global Legal Insights, *Energy Laws and Regulations 2025: Zambia*. https://www.globallegalinsights.com/practice-areas/energy-laws-and-regulations/zambia/
- The 2023/24 drought (worst in five decades) collapsed reservoir levels and forced ZESCO into load shedding **of up to 21 hours per day.** Source: Pulitzer Center reporting. https://pulitzercenter.org/stories/zambias-power-shortages-worsen-drought-deepens

> **Framing:** Zambia bet on "clean" hydropower and got crushed by a climate-driven drought. The lesson is *not* "go back to coal" — but "diversify with firm power," which today still means some gas. This is the lived experience of the audience.

## 4. Natural Gas as a Transition Fuel — the engineering middle path

- **Natural gas emits ~50–60% less CO₂ than coal** for the same electricity output. Source: Atlantic Council, *Natural gas has a small but important role in Africa's energy transition*. https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/in-depth-research-reports/report/natural-gas-has-a-small-but-important-role-in-africas-energy-transition/
- Coal-to-gas switching avoided **~500 Mt CO₂ globally between 2010 and 2019.**
- Africa holds **~13% of the world's proven natural gas reserves**, with major recent discoveries in Mozambique, Tanzania, Egypt, Senegal, Mauritania, South Africa.
- **Honest caveat:** Methane (the primary component of natural gas) leaks during production and is ~86× more potent than CO₂ over a 20-year window. The "50–60% better than coal" advantage erodes substantially if methane leak rates exceed ~3%. Source: same Atlantic Council report.

> **Framing:** Don't oversell gas as a "clean" solution. Frame it as the realistic *bridge* — better than coal, faster to deploy than nuclear, firm in a way solar+wind alone aren't yet.

## 5. The Materials Reality — there is no renewable economy without fossil fuels (today)

- **A single wind turbine requires ~900 tonnes of steel, ~2,500 tonnes of concrete, and ~45 tonnes of non-recyclable plastic.**
- **Steel and cement each account for ~7% of global CO₂ emissions.** Steel blast furnaces operate at ~1,100°C; cement kilns at ~1,400°C — temperatures currently reached almost exclusively via fossil fuels.
- Source: MIT Climate Portal, *Does the steel and concrete needed to build renewable energy cancel out the benefits?* https://climate.mit.edu/ask-mit/does-steel-and-concrete-needed-build-renewable-energy-cancel-out-benefits
- **Critical honest caveat:** MIT's actual answer is **NO**, the emissions from building renewables do *not* cancel the benefits — a wind turbine pays back its construction emissions in roughly 6 months of operation. **Do not misuse this stat to imply renewables aren't worth building.** The right framing is: *the transition itself runs on fossil-fuel-powered industry, so cutting them off abruptly cuts off the very supply chain that builds the alternative.*

> **Framing:** This is a "you have to use the ladder to climb out of the well" argument. Be careful — it's easy to misuse. The MIT data is the honest version.

## 6. Energy and Life Expectancy — the development curve

- Below ~40 MWh per person per year, **more energy access strongly correlates with longer life expectancy.** Above that threshold the relationship decouples. Source: Jackson, *Human well-being and per capita energy use*, Ecosphere 2022. https://esajournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ecs2.3978
- Hans Rosling's Gapminder work: ~80% of the variance in national health outcomes is explained by income, and energy access is the primary driver of income growth in pre-industrial economies.

> **Framing:** Most rich-world climate advocacy is *above* the decoupling threshold — cutting energy use there has small human cost. Below the threshold (where most Africans live), every additional kWh translates directly into clinics, refrigerated vaccines, lit classrooms, water pumps, life-years.

## What I'm NOT going to argue (guardrails for honest steelman)

- ✗ "Climate change isn't real" — it is, and my own deck showed it.
- ✗ "Renewables don't work" — they do, and they're falling in cost faster than anyone predicted.
- ✗ "Africa should burn more coal" — coal-specific. Gas + carefully expanded grid + accelerated renewables is the actual case.
- ✗ Cherry-picked or unsourced stats. If I can't cite it, it doesn't go in.

## What the deck WILL argue

- ✅ The cost-benefit picture my original deck showed was incomplete: it counted future climate deaths but not present energy-poverty deaths.
- ✅ The most climate-vulnerable countries are *also* the most energy-poor, and the cheapest fastest fix for energy poverty today still includes fossil fuels (specifically gas).
- ✅ A managed transition — gas as bridge, renewables aggressively scaled, grids made firmer — saves more lives than an abrupt shutdown.
- ✅ Zambia is the worked example: hydropower-only failed in a drought; the answer is *more* energy sources, not fewer.
